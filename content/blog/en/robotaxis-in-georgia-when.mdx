<p>No robotaxi operator has announced a launch date for Georgia, and as of mid-2026 the country has no dedicated autonomous-vehicle framework, so the honest answer is a conditional one: robotaxis reach Georgia after three preconditions line up, regulation that permits driverless operation, an operator or vehicle platform willing to enter a small market, and local fleets ready to run the service on the ground. Each precondition has a visible signal. Regulation follows the UN's new global rules for automated driving adopted in 2026. Operator interest follows the international expansion already underway, with <a href="https://cnevpost.com/2026/02/27/baidu-apollo-go-robotaxi-300000-weekly-rides-expands-to-south-korea/">Apollo Go moving into South Korea</a> as the first big export case. Local readiness is the one factor Georgian taxi companies control today, and it is the cheapest of the three to start on. This article maps all three preconditions, the signals that show each one moving, and the preparation that pays off regardless of the date.</p>

<h2 id="what-does-the-global-map-look-like">Where does the global rollout stand?</h2>

<p>The leaders are scaling fast in their home markets: <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/27/waymo-skyrocketing-ridership-in-one-chart/">Waymo runs 500,000 paid rides per week across 10 US cities</a> and Apollo Go has passed <a href="https://ir.baidu.com/news-releases/news-release-details/baidu-announces-first-quarter-2026-results/">22 million cumulative rides across roughly 27 cities</a>. Goldman Sachs Research expects the global fleet to reach <a href="https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/robotaxis-to-become-a-400-billion-dollar-market-in-2035">about one million vehicles by 2030</a>. Expansion so far follows demand density and regulatory clarity, which puts large cities first and countries like Georgia in the second wave; the full operator picture is in our review of <a href="/en/blog/global-robotaxi-market-2026">the global robotaxi market</a>.</p>

<h2 id="what-regulation-would-georgia-need">What regulation would Georgia need?</h2>

<p>Georgia currently regulates taxis through municipal permit systems, Tbilisi's category A permits among them, with no announced framework for vehicles without drivers. The building blocks now exist internationally: the UNECE working party adopted a <a href="https://unece.org/sustainable-development/press/un-announces-global-regulation-facilitate-safe-introduction-self">draft global technical regulation for automated driving systems in January 2026</a>, built on a safety-case approach, and the EU is preparing <a href="https://www.taylorwessing.com/en/insights-and-events/insights/2026/02/legal-frameworks-for-autonomous-driving-and-teledriving">type-approval changes with regulatory sandboxes from 2026</a>. Georgia has a history of adopting international vehicle standards rather than writing its own from scratch, so the realistic legislative path is an adaptation of the UNECE framework. The details of how these rules work are in <a href="/en/blog/autonomous-taxi-safety-regulation">autonomous taxi regulation</a>.</p>

<h2 id="why-would-an-operator-choose-georgia">Why would an operator choose Georgia at all?</h2>

<p>Three properties work in the country's favor. Tbilisi is a compact capital where one geofence covers most demand. The taxi market is deep: <a href="https://gbc.ge/en/news/Economics-news/184-of-drivers-in-tbilisi-are-taxi-drivers">18.4% of all drivers in Tbilisi are taxi drivers, with more than 85,000 registered taxis</a>, so ride-hailing habits are universal. And the market runs through apps already, with Bolt dominant, which means demand is digital and measurable. Against that stand low fares, around <a href="https://thegeorgianguide.com/practical-info/bolt-georgia-guide/">1.5 to 2 GEL per kilometer</a>, which delay the pure cost crossover, as our breakdown of robotaxi economics shows. The likely entry model is therefore a partnership: a global driving system, a local fleet operator, shared economics.</p>

<h2 id="what-signals-should-you-watch">Which signals say the clock has started?</h2>

<ul>
<li>A Georgian regulatory pilot or sandbox for automated driving, following the UNECE framework.</li>
<li>A ride-hailing platform active in the region announcing AV partnerships for secondary markets.</li>
<li>An AV manufacturer opening sales or licensing to fleet buyers rather than running everything itself.</li>
<li>Charging infrastructure programs sized for commercial fleets in Tbilisi.</li>
</ul>

<p>Any two of these together would compress the timeline sharply. None of them are announced today, which is exactly why the preparation window exists.</p>

<h2 id="what-can-a-taxi-company-do-now">What can a Georgian taxi company do before launch?</h2>

<p>The work that takes years is operational, and none of it requires owning an autonomous vehicle: measuring the cost baseline, mapping demand zones, planning depot power, and moving dispatch onto a platform that can later run <a href="/en/blog/integrate-autonomous-vehicles-existing-taxi-fleet">a hybrid human and autonomous fleet</a>. The broader modernization story, from switchboards to apps to autonomy, is traced in <a href="/en/blog/taxi-business-digitalization-georgia">taxi digitalization in Georgia</a>.</p>

<p>aiTAXI is a robotaxi fleet management platform by aiNOW (Tbilisi, Georgia), built for this preparation phase: the <a href="/en">robotaxi fleet management platform</a> is in early access, and the pilot program recruits Georgian taxi companies that want the fleet layer running before the first autonomous vehicle clears customs.</p>

<h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>

<h3 id="faq-1">Has any company announced robotaxis for Georgia?</h3>
<p>No. As of mid-2026 no operator has announced a Georgian launch, and no dedicated AV framework has been announced by the government. Everything in this article is preconditions and signals, not dates.</p>

<h3 id="faq-2">Could Georgia skip ahead of bigger markets?</h3>
<p>Small markets have done it with other technology waves, and a compact capital plus app-first riders helps. It would take a deliberate regulatory pilot and an operator partnership; neither exists yet.</p>

<h3 id="faq-3">Will low Georgian fares block robotaxis?</h3>
<p>They delay the cost crossover rather than block it. As hardware and operations costs fall with global scale, the economics reach lower-fare markets a few years behind the leaders.</p>

<h3 id="faq-4">What should a fleet owner do first?</h3>
<p>Measure the current operation: cost per kilometer, demand by zone and hour, depot capacity. That baseline decides whether and when autonomous vehicles make sense, and it makes any future partnership conversation concrete.</p>
