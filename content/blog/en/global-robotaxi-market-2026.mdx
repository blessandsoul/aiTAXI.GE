<p>The global robotaxi market in 2026 is a working industry with four leading operators and measurable volume. Waymo delivers <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/27/waymo-skyrocketing-ridership-in-one-chart/">500,000 paid rides per week across 10 US cities</a> and targets one million weekly rides by the end of the year. Baidu's Apollo Go has completed <a href="https://ir.baidu.com/news-releases/news-release-details/baidu-announces-first-quarter-2026-results/">more than 22 million cumulative rides</a> across roughly 27 cities. Tesla runs an unsupervised service across the Austin metro, and Amazon's Zoox has carried over 350,000 riders in Las Vegas. Goldman Sachs Research sizes the market at <a href="https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/robotaxis-to-become-a-400-billion-dollar-market-in-2035">roughly $415 billion by 2035</a>, with the global fleet growing to about one million vehicles by 2030. The race is no longer about whether driverless taxis work; it is about who operates them in each city. Four operators dominate the volume today, each with a different vehicle strategy and expansion map, and their choices set the template that every later market, large or small, will inherit.</p>

<h2 id="what-does-waymos-growth-show">What does Waymo's growth curve show?</h2>

<p>The steepness is the story: from roughly 50,000 weekly rides in May 2024 to <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/27/waymo-skyrocketing-ridership-in-one-chart/">500,000 by March 2026</a>, a tenfold climb in under two years, with a stated goal of one million weekly rides by the end of 2026. Growth came from city-by-city expansion across the US Sun Belt. The safety record scaled with it: across 56.7 million rider-only miles Waymo reports <a href="https://waymo.com/safety/impact/">92% fewer serious-injury crashes than human benchmarks</a>, the dataset that anchors the whole industry's argument to regulators, covered further in <a href="/en/blog/autonomous-taxi-safety-regulation">safety and regulation</a>.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-apollo-go-doing-differently">What is Apollo Go doing differently?</h2>

<p>Apollo Go built volume through Chinese cities with supportive regulation and dense demand, then started exporting: <a href="https://cnevpost.com/2026/02/27/baidu-apollo-go-robotaxi-300000-weekly-rides-expands-to-south-korea/">300,000 weekly rides as of February 2026 and an announced expansion into South Korea</a>. Its first quarter of 2026 delivered 3.2 million fully driverless rides, growth of more than 120% year over year. Apollo Go demonstrates the international playbook: a driving system developed at home, deployed abroad through local partnerships, which is exactly the pattern smaller countries should expect when the wave reaches them.</p>

<h2 id="what-about-tesla-and-zoox">Where do Tesla and Zoox stand?</h2>

<p>Tesla launched in Austin in June 2025 with safety monitors, removed them for part of the fleet from January 2026, and by June 2026 ran unsupervised service across the whole Austin metro, though with <a href="https://electrek.co/2026/03/31/tesla-expands-unsupervised-robotaxi-service-area-still-only-handful-vehicles/">only around 20 vehicles operating</a>. The bet is different from Waymo's: camera-only hardware on a mass-produced consumer car, which would make fleet supply cheap if the approach proves out at scale.</p>

<p>Zoox went the opposite way with a purpose-built vehicle that has no steering wheel at all. Its Las Vegas service has driven <a href="https://electrek.co/2026/03/24/zoox-expands-current-service-area-and-is-bringing-its-purpose-built-robotaxi-to-two-new-cities/">nearly two million autonomous miles and carried more than 350,000 riders</a>, with expansion announced for San Francisco, Austin and Miami. Four operators, four vehicle strategies: retrofitted premium cars, mass consumer cars, purpose-built pods, and licensed stacks.</p>

<h2 id="what-do-the-forecasts-say">What do the forecasts say about the next decade?</h2>

<p>Goldman Sachs Research projects the US robotaxi market at <a href="https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/robotaxis-to-become-a-400-billion-dollar-market-in-2035">$19 billion in 2030 and the global market at roughly $415 billion in 2035</a>, with the worldwide fleet reaching about one million vehicles in 2030 and six million in 2035. Forecasts differ on timing, but the direction is shared, and the unit-level argument behind it, the cost curve of removing the driver, is laid out in our piece on <a href="/en/blog/robotaxi-economics-cost-per-km">robotaxi unit economics</a>.</p>

<h2 id="what-does-this-mean-for-small-markets">What does this mean for markets like Georgia?</h2>

<p>The operators above expand where regulation, mapping and fleet economics line up, which means secondary markets join the map in the second wave, through partnerships with local fleets rather than direct entry. For a taxi company in a country like Georgia the practical questions are the preconditions and the timing, mapped in <a href="/en/blog/robotaxis-in-georgia-when">when robotaxis reach Georgia</a>, and the operational readiness described in <a href="/en/blog/what-is-a-robotaxi">what a robotaxi is and how it works</a>.</p>

The platform is in early access with a pilot program for Georgian operators.</p>

<h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>

<h3 id="faq-1">Which company leads the robotaxi market in 2026?</h3>
<p>By weekly volume, Waymo leads the Western market with 500,000 paid rides per week, while Apollo Go leads on cumulative rides with more than 22 million and the largest city count.</p>

<h3 id="faq-2">Is the robotaxi market profitable yet?</h3>
<p>Operators are still investing ahead of profit at fleet level, and the forecasts, including Goldman's $415 billion by 2035, price in scale that has not arrived yet. Unit economics improve with every increase in utilization and fleet size.</p>

<h3 id="faq-3">Why do operators expand city by city instead of everywhere?</h3>
<p>Each city needs mapping, a safety case, local approval and a depot. The playbook is to saturate one geofence, prove the record, then replicate.</p>

<h3 id="faq-4">Will these operators come to small countries directly?</h3>
<p>The pattern so far is partnerships: the driving system arrives from a global player, while local companies contribute fleets, depots and market knowledge. That makes local operational readiness the scarce asset.</p>
